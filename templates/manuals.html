This page serves as a way to help provide information to new users.
If you are planning to invest, only invest an amount that you are willing to lose. Generally, it is less risky to invest in ETFs as it is a bundle of stocks. If one stock goes down in price, it will not affect you as much as it would if you invested into a singular stock.


Lingo:

A dividend is the amount of money investors earn when the company they are investiing in earns a profit. A certain amount of the profit earned by the company is given to shareholders as a dividend, and any amount that has not been distributed is used for the business to reinvest in itself.
Ordinary dividends are taxed at ordinary income tax rates.

A person may choose to purchase ETFs exchange-traded funds instead of purchasing the stocks of a specific company. An ETF is like a bundle of similar companies a person can choose to invest in, rather than investing in a singular company. In a way, ETFs can offer more security for the investor, but may result in a lower reward.

Capital Gains:
    Short term: they are usually taxed as ordinary income tax rates 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37%
    Long term: they are the profits from selling assets owned for more than a year. They are usually taxes at lower long term gain tax rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)

Interest Income:
    Most of our interest income is taxed at ordinary income rates. Interest may be exempt from federal taxes including municipal bond interest, private activity bonds, and exempt-interest dividends.
